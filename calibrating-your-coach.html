I was once brought in to work with a business that was struggling to develop senior talent. They had a coaching program already in place — external coaches, credentialed people, doing what looked like real work.

But the results weren't there. The sessions were happening. The hours were being logged. And almost nothing was changing.

I sat in on three sessions before I understood the problem. The coaches were good. The executives were engaged. But every session started the same way: fifteen minutes of catch-up. Who are you, really? What's the actual pressure you're under? What did we cover last time that still matters?

Not because the coaches were lazy. Because no one had ever given them the context that would have made those fifteen minutes unnecessary. Nobody had said: here is who this person is, here is what they're carrying, here is where they need the most pressure and where they need the most space.

So the coaches filled that gap the only way they could — by asking. Every session. From scratch.

By the time they got to the real work, the window was already closing.

That's what this lesson is about. Not methodology. Not excavation. Setup. The short, consequential work you do before you ever type a word to your coach.

Context Changes Everything

Your coach is intelligent. It will read what you give it, hold it, build on it. But it cannot know what you haven't told it. And without context, even the most sophisticated coaching partner falls back on generalities.

It asks questions it wouldn't need to ask. It gives guidance calibrated for a generic writer, not for you. It doesn't know whether you're working from fifty manuscript pages or from scattered notes. It doesn't know whether you're writing literary fiction or a thriller. It doesn't know whether the character you're bringing to the excavation is fully formed or half-imagined.

Context changes everything. Not abstractly — specifically. When your coach knows your genre, it knows the conventions your character lives inside. When it knows where you are in the writing process, it can calibrate how hard to push and when to move carefully. When it knows what's giving you trouble, it knows exactly where to focus from the opening exchange.

What I'm asking you to do here takes five minutes. The return on those five minutes runs across every session in this course.

The Methodology

Why Context Changes Everything

A coach without context gives generic guidance. The five minutes you spend on the Pre-Work form determines the quality of every session that follows.

The Fifteen Minutes You're Buying Back

Think about what it would be like to work with a human writing coach — someone you'd hired, someone with real expertise. The first thing they'd do before your first session is ask you some questions. What are you working on? Where are you in the process? What's giving you trouble?

Without that context, even the best coach is guessing. They're giving you generic guidance instead of targeted guidance. They're asking questions they wouldn't need to ask if they'd known going in. Your AI coach works exactly the same way.
