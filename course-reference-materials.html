Course Glossary · Print & Keep
STRATUM — Precise Terms, Not Interchangeable Ones
These are precise terms, not interchangeable ones. Review this glossary before Lesson 1 and return to it whenever a term appears in a coaching session that you cannot immediately define.
Core Methodology
Excavation Not Fabrication The Foundational Principle
Excavation is the act of finding psychological truth that already exists inside your character. Fabrication is the act of constructing backstory that fits a plot. Fabricated characters are plausible. Excavated characters are true. The difference is felt, not argued.
Signal of fabrication: "This would work for the story." Signal of excavation: "Of course. I always knew this about them."
The Excavation Ladder Course Architecture
The eight-layer progressive model at the heart of STRATUM. Each rung moves deeper into your character's psychology: from observable behavior (L1) through hidden belief (L2), origin wound (L3), distortion (L4), want and need (L5), emotional fuel (L6), domain mapping (L7), and integrated architecture (L8). No layer can be completed accurately without the one beneath it.
The Felt Sense of Discovery Diagnostic Signal
The internal recognition that occurs when you have found something true about your character rather than invented it. It does not feel like inspiration or invention. It feels like recognition — a sudden, quiet certainty: "Of course. That's where that came from. I've always known this about them." If what you find feels constructed, keep excavating.
Psychological Architecture The Complete Structure
The complete, causally locked system of your character's inner life — all eight layers functioning as a single coherent whole. When complete, every choice your character makes flows inevitably from one source: the wound.
Lesson 1 — The Surface Pattern
Anchor Behavior L1 Primary Deliverable
The single recurring behavior that anchors your character's psychology. Must meet all four criteria: Specific (precise enough to film), Visible (observable, not internal), Recurring (appears across multiple contexts), Consequential (shapes the story's direction).
Observer Mode L1 Cognitive Stance
The discipline of recording only what is visible — what a camera could film — without interpreting motivation or assigning meaning. Observer mode is deliberately restricted in Lesson 1. Interpretation begins in Lesson 2.
Lesson 2 — The Hidden Truth
The Therapist's Question L2 Core Tool
"What would someone have to believe — about themselves, about other people, or about how the world works — for this behavior to feel like the only reasonable response?" The precision of this question matters. It does not ask what caused the behavior. It asks what belief makes the behavior feel necessary and inevitable.
Hidden Truth L2 Primary Deliverable
The belief operating beneath the character's conscious awareness that makes their behavior feel not just reasonable, but inevitable. Distinct from the conscious justification: the hidden truth is what actually drives them, not what they would say if asked.
Conscious justification: what the character would say. Hidden truth: what you would never hear them say — but that explains everything.
Causal Lock L2 Test of Precision
The tight causal connection between hidden truth and anchor behavior, stated as: "Because my character believes [X], they must [Y behavior]." If the behavior feels inevitable given the belief, the lock is tight. If it requires explanation, the belief may not be precise enough yet.
Lesson 3 — The Formative Wound
Formative Wound L3 Primary Deliverable
The specific experience — or specific accumulation of moments — that made the hidden truth the only reasonable conclusion. A wound is not a category (abandonment, betrayal) — it is a scene. The moment a child looked for an adult and found no one. The night a parent failed to show up. Wounds don't need to be catastrophic. They need to be specific and causally connected to the belief.
Wound Proportionality L3 Principle
The wound should be proportional to the belief — not to what would make dramatic backstory. The smallest experience sufficient to create the belief is often the most authentic. Ask: "What's the minimum that would have been enough to produce this conviction?"
Lesson 4 — The Lie
The Lie L4 Primary Deliverable
The distorted conclusion the character drew from the wound — a self-referential statement about identity, worth, or obligation. Distinct from the hidden truth: the hidden truth is an observation about the world or others; the lie is a conclusion about the self. The lie is what the story must crack open.
Hidden truth (L2): "The people I love cannot protect themselves." Lie (L4): "I am solely responsible for everyone's survival."
Lesson 5 — Want vs. Need
The Want L5 — The False Pursuit
The psychological strategy the lie produces — what the character pursues to feel safe, validated, or whole. Not the plot goal. The want is built on the lie and cannot deliver what the character actually needs. Pursuing it confirms the lie and blocks access to the need.
The Need L5 — What the Wound Took Away
What was absent in the moment the wound formed — what the character still requires to heal. The need is almost always simple: safety, belonging, permission to fail, love without conditions. The character cannot simply choose the need because it threatens the lie, which feels like survival.
Structural Conflict L5 Test
The want and need must be mutually exclusive — pursuing one at full intensity must actively block access to the other. If both can coexist, you don't have the right pair yet. The structural conflict between want and need is the engine of your story's arc.
