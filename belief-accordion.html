Every writer brings at least one belief with them. A belief that explains why their characters aren't working. It might be quiet. It might sound reasonable. It might have been handed to you by a workshop, a rejection letter, or a writing teacher whose voice you've never stopped hearing.

Mine was the genre one: I need to know where the plot is going before my characters can fully exist. When I sat down to write fiction, I kept waiting for the story architecture to tell me who my characters were. I had the method backwards the whole time. The belief came from the novels I loved: Cussler, Brown, Clancy. Every one of them runs on plot. I absorbed the structure without realizing I was also absorbing an assumption about which came first.

"If I could just find the right image — the right detail — my characters would come alive. I just need to show more, not tell." ▾

This one often comes from years of being told to show, don't tell. It produces beautiful surfaces and empty interiors. The writer becomes expert at depicting behavior... and loses access to the logic underneath it.

"My characters aren't coming together because I don't have enough plot yet. Once I know what happens, they'll know what to say." ▾

This one often grows in writers trained on genre fiction — thrillers, mysteries, adventure. It treats character as a function of plot. The character waits to be activated by events rather than arriving with something already at stake.

"Some writers are just naturally good at this. I'm not one of them." ▾

This one is the most corrosive because it makes the problem permanent. It turns a skills gap into a personality verdict. Once you believe it, every flat scene becomes evidence — and you stop looking for a method.
