Let me tell you about a session I ran early in my corporate coaching career.

I was working with a high-performing executive — mid-forties, technically brilliant, led a division of three hundred people. His company had brought me in because despite his results, he was bleeding talent. Good people kept leaving, and no one could say exactly why.

I spent a day observing him. I watched him in meetings, in one-on-ones, in the hallway between those two things. And something kept surfacing. Any time a direct report brought him a problem, he solved it. Immediately. Completely. He didn't ask questions, he didn't explore options, he just fixed it and moved on. Fast. Efficient. Decisive.

On paper, that looks like strong leadership. Except his people told me — quietly, individually, in the way people tell you things they won't say out loud in a room — that working for him felt like being managed, not developed. They said they never got to think for themselves. That he didn't trust them. That their judgment never got to count for anything.

He was baffled by that feedback. Genuinely confused. From his perspective, he was helping.

That's a perfect illustration of what we're dealing with in this lesson. Because the behavior was clear — in Lesson 1, we'd call it the anchor behavior. The hidden truth was visible too — in Lesson 2, we'd find the belief that made rescuing people feel not just reasonable, but necessary.

But neither of those layers explained the question that actually mattered.

Why did he need to be the one who solved it?

That's what Lesson 3 is about. Not what he does. Not what he believes. What happened to him that made this belief feel true.

In Lesson 1, I asked you to be a documentary filmmaker. You stayed on the surface — observing, cataloging, recording behavior with precision. In Lesson 2, you became a therapist. You applied the therapist's question and found the belief operating beneath the behavior. The hidden truth that makes your character's actions feel not just understandable, but inevitable.

Today, you shift roles one more time.

Today, you become a diagnostician.

Not a filmmaker watching what happens. Not a therapist asking what it means. A diagnostician tracing a belief back to its origin — the specific experience that made this belief feel true, possibly the only time in this character's life when it could have felt this permanently, unmovably true.

That experience is the formative wound.

What the Wound Actually Is

Now, I want to stop and address something before we go further. Because the word "wound" does something to writers. The moment I say formative wound, a certain part of the brain activates, and it starts reaching. It reaches for the dramatic. The catastrophic. The unmistakable.

And I understand the instinct. If your character is going to carry something for the length of a novel — if it's going to drive their behavior in chapter three and chapter twenty-seven and the climax — it seems like whatever created it should be proportionally significant. Heavy enough to justify the weight.

Here's what thirty-five years of sitting with real human beings taught me.

The experiences that shape us most profoundly are almost never the catastrophic ones.

Catastrophic events are traumatic. They're damaging. But they're also legible. When something unmistakably terrible happens to a child, the child knows something terrible happened. The event has a name. The story is readable. The conclusion is available.

The experiences that create the deepest, most persistent beliefs are the ones that were confusing. The chronic pattern. The repeated absence. The ordinary moment that shouldn't have mattered but somehow did. The door that stayed closed. The conversation that kept not happening. The thing that was almost there, almost enough, and then wasn't.

Those experiences are confusing precisely because they don't announce themselves as significant. And because they can't be named, they get explained instead. The child can't say "my father doesn't know how to show love." So they explain it as something about themselves. Something like: what I do isn't worth stopping for.

That explanation becomes a belief. And that belief, if it's real — if it's specific — will drive behavior for the rest of the character's life.

Katniss: Finding the Wound

Let me show you what that looks like with Katniss.

In Lesson 1, we established her anchor behavior: she volunteers. She steps between danger and the people she loves. Protector, again and again, across different contexts and different relationships.

In Lesson 2, we found the hidden truth: the belief that makes that behavior feel not like a choice but like a compulsion. If I am not the one standing between the people I love and what threatens them, they will be destroyed.

Now, in Lesson 3, we ask the diagnostician's question. Not what does she do. Not what does she believe. What happened to her that made this belief feel permanently, foundationally true?

The answer the methodology points toward isn't a single dramatic event. It's Katniss at approximately eleven years old, in the immediate aftermath of her father's death, watching her mother disappear into grief. Not leaving physically — staying in the house, present in the room. But gone. Unreachable. And Prim going hungry.

The wound isn't her father's death. The wound is the days and weeks that followed, when the child who needed a parent looked for one and found empty space. When she reached and found nothing. When she learned — not from a single moment but from the accumulated weight of those weeks — that if she didn't act, no one would.

That's the wound. And notice its proportionality. It's not the most dramatic thing that could have happened to her. It's the most specific thing. The thing that made this belief — not grief in general, not childhood loss in general, but this precise conviction about what happens if she doesn't stand up — feel true.

The Three-Part Discovery

The methodology I use for finding the wound works in three stages, and they build on each other.

The first stage is deductive reasoning. You're not searching all of human experience for a wound that fits. You're working backward from the hidden truth you found in Lesson 2. You're asking: what kind of experience would produce this specific belief? Not what caused suffering — what caused this conclusion. That's a much narrower question, and it narrows the field considerably.

The second stage is intuitive visualization. Stop asking what would fit. Close your eyes and ask: when you imagine your character's past, what surfaces? Not what should be there — what arrives? An image. A room. A face. A specific quality of light or silence. Don't evaluate it yet. Just notice what keeps showing up.

I've watched writers in this stage experience something I can only describe as recognition — a quiet, slightly startling certainty that something has surfaced which was already there. Not invented. Found. With construction, you feel satisfaction. With discovery, you feel something closer to surprise. Something like: of course. Of course that's where it came from.

If you feel that — trust it. That's the signal.

The third stage is Socratic questioning. This is what your coaching session is designed for. Your coach will take everything you've brought from the first two stages and challenge it. Not to be difficult — to be precise. The wound that survives the Socratic stage is the one that needs no argument. The one where the connection between what happened and what was concluded is immediate and inevitable.

The Lock Test for This Lesson

There's a test I want you to apply to every candidate wound, and it's the same structure as the lock test from Lesson 2.

In Lesson 2, you wrote: Because my character believes X, they must Y.

In Lesson 3, you write: Because of this wound, my character concluded X.

If you have to explain the connection — if you have to walk me through why this experience would produce this belief — the lock isn't tight enough. The real wound doesn't require a logical argument. It requires only a witness. You show me the wound. I see the belief. That's the test.

When that sentence reads with the same inevitability as the Lesson 2 lock — when you feel not just that it's coherent but that it couldn't be any other way — you're done.

Supplemental · Writing Psychology The Wound That Doesn't Look Like a Wound

I want to ask you something before we get into this.

Think about the most psychologically complex character you've ever encountered in a novel or a film. The one you couldn't stop thinking about after you finished. The one who felt completely real.

Now think about what made them that way.

I'd be willing to bet it wasn't a dramatic trauma. It wasn't an earthquake or a kidnapping or a near-death experience. It was something quieter. Something that, if you described it to someone at a dinner party, they might not immediately understand why it mattered so much.

That's not a coincidence. That's craft. And most writers are working against it without knowing it.

My name is Ted Baker, and for thirty-five years I worked as an executive coach and behavioral diagnostician with Fortune 500 companies. I helped organizations understand why their highest performers behaved the way they did under pressure — what beliefs were driving them, and where those beliefs came from.

When I left that world and sat down to write my own novel, I brought those diagnostic tools with me. And one of the first things I noticed was this: the writers who were struggling with flat characters weren't struggling because they hadn't given their characters enough backstory. They were struggling because they'd given their characters the wrong kind of wound.

The Wrong Kind of Wound

When most writers think about a character's formative experience — the thing that shaped who they became — they reach for the catastrophic. The house fire. The violent parent. The sudden death. The public humiliation at scale.

And I understand why. Those events feel significant. They feel like they justify the weight of a character's psychological burden. If your character is going to carry something heavy for four hundred pages, it seems like the inciting experience should be proportionally heavy.

But here's what I learned from decades of sitting across from real human beings and tracing their behavior back to its origins.

The experiences that shape us most profoundly are almost never the catastrophic ones.

They're the ones that happened in an ordinary moment, in an ordinary room, with someone who mattered to us. And in that moment, something small went wrong.

A father who came to every game but never said the words. A mother whose praise always arrived with a qualifier. A teacher who called on everyone else. A room full of people who stayed quiet when they should have spoken up. A door that closed. A conversation that never happened.

These are the wounds that stick. Not because they were dramatic. Because they were confusing.

With a catastrophic event, the child knows what happened. The story is legible. The wound is visible.

But with the quiet wound — the chronic absence, the subtle dismissal, the repeated pattern of almost — the child doesn't have language for it. They can't name it. And because they can't name it, they explain it.

They explain it as something about themselves.

That explanation becomes a belief. And that belief becomes the engine of the character.

That's what makes a complex fictional character feel like a real person. Not the size of what happened to them. The specificity of what they concluded about themselves because of it.

Two Failure Modes

I've worked with a lot of writers who are excavating their characters' formative experiences, and I see two predictable failure modes.

The first is what I call the movie-of-the-week wound. It's dramatic, it's legible, it's emotionally clear. The problem is it's also generic. Readers recognize it as a narrative device. It doesn't surprise them. It confirms what they already expected about how storytelling works.

The second failure mode is actually the mirror image. Writers who are self-aware enough to avoid the obvious trauma sometimes swing too far in the other direction and land on something so mundane it doesn't carry enough psychological weight. The wound needs to be quiet — but it also needs to be charged.

The sweet spot is specificity.

Not "his father was emotionally unavailable." That's a category, not a wound.

The wound is the Tuesday evening when the father came home, and the child had done something they were proud of, and showed it, and the father glanced at it and set it on the counter and turned on the television. That specific Tuesday. That specific glance. That specific counter.

Because the child didn't conclude "my father is emotionally unavailable." The child concluded something about themselves. Something like: the things I create are not worth stopping for.

That's a belief. And that belief — if it's real, if it's specific, if it fits — will drive behavior for the rest of the character's life in ways that feel both inevitable and surprising. Which is exactly what great characterization does.

Inevitable because of course they behave this way — look at what happened to them. Surprising because you couldn't have predicted this specific expression of it.

A Practical Test

Here's a practical test I use, and I want you to use it on your own characters.

Take whatever formative wound you've identified or are considering for your character. Then ask yourself two questions.

First: Is this wound something a camera could have recorded? Not the emotion. The event. The specific, observable moment. If you can't see it as a scene — if it's still a category or a pattern rather than a moment — you haven't found the wound yet. You've found the context around it.

Second: What did the child in that moment need that they didn't get? Not what happened to them. What was absent. What they reached for and found empty space.

That absence is where the belief forms.

Because human beings — and fictional characters who feel like human beings — don't build their psychological architecture around what was done to them. They build it around what they needed and didn't receive.

The father didn't hit him. He just didn't stop. And the child needed him to stop.

That's the wound. That's the origin of the belief. And that's the engine of the story.

The Instinct Already Knows

The writers I've seen make the greatest breakthroughs in character work are not the ones who invented the most elaborate backstories. They're the ones who learned to trust the quiet, specific, almost-embarrassingly-small moment that kept surfacing in their mind when they thought about their character.

The instinct usually knows before the conscious mind catches up.

The work isn't manufacturing something dramatic enough to justify the character's psychology. The work is excavating what's already there.

Supplemental · The Methodology Discovery vs. Invention: How Writers Know the Difference

There's a question I ask writers when they bring me a character they've been struggling with.

I ask them: Did you find this character, or did you build them?

It sounds philosophical. It isn't. It's diagnostic. And the answer tells me almost everything I need to know about why the character is or isn't working.

My name is Ted Baker. I spent thirty-five years in behavioral diagnostics and executive coaching before I became a writing instructor. And the distinction I just described — finding versus building — is the single most important concept I've carried from that career into the work I do with fiction writers.

Finding vs. Building

When I was still in the corporate world, I was occasionally brought in to assess executives who were described as high-potential but somehow self-limiting. People who were technically excellent but kept hitting the same ceiling. Who made the same category of decision under pressure, even when they could see it wasn't serving them.

My job was to figure out why.

And what I learned very quickly was that the wrong approach was to look at who they appeared to be and construct a psychological explanation that fit the observed behavior. That's building. You're manufacturing a narrative that's coherent and plausible and satisfying.

The right approach was to keep asking questions until the person revealed something they hadn't planned to reveal. Until a pattern surfaced that neither of us had been looking for. Until the real thing emerged.

That's finding.

The difference between those two approaches produced completely different results. The first gave me a character — a coherent, believable, internally consistent explanation. The second gave me a person — something surprising, specific, and true.

Writers face exactly this choice every time they sit down to develop a character. And most of the time, without realizing it, they build.

How You Know You're Building

Here's how you know you're building rather than finding.

The character's psychology is tidy. Everything connects cleanly. The backstory explains the behavior in a way that feels like a closed loop. There are no jagged edges. There are no elements that surprise even you. The character behaves exactly as their backstory would predict.

That tidiness is the tell. Real people — and characters who feel like real people — are not tidy. They contain contradictions they can't fully account for. They know something is driving them but can't quite name it. Their behavior occasionally surprises even themselves.

When a character surprises their own author, that's almost always a sign that something real has been found.

Now let me be direct about something, because I think it's important.

Invention is not a moral failure. Every writer invents. The question is whether you stop there.

Invention is the starting hypothesis. It's the first draft of the character's psychology. You ask yourself what kind of wound would create this belief, and you land on something plausible. That's not the end of the process. That's the beginning of the excavation.

The work is to take that hypothesis and pressure-test it. To ask: does this feel constructed, or does it feel found? To sit with it long enough that either it deepens into something true, or it reveals itself as a convenient fiction that needs to be replaced.

A Concrete Distinction

Here's a concrete distinction I use.

When you've invented something, you can explain it. You can walk me through the logic. You can describe how A led to B led to C. The explanation is smooth and available.

When you've discovered something, you often can't fully explain it yet. You know it's right before you can articulate why. The connection feels immediate rather than constructed. You recognize it more than you reason toward it.

That recognition — that moment of this is it, I don't know how I know but I know — is the signal you've found something real.

Consider Katniss Everdeen from The Hunger Games.

If a writer sat down to build a character who would volunteer to replace their younger sibling in a death match, they might construct something like this: loving older sibling, strong protective instinct, family history of sacrifice. Plausible. Coherent. Forgettable.

What Suzanne Collins gave us is something that feels found. Katniss doesn't volunteer from love, primarily. She volunteers from something that looks like love but functions more like a compulsion. An inability to be the person who didn't act. A self-concept so tied to protection that the alternative — watching and doing nothing — would constitute a kind of self-annihilation.

That's not constructed. That's excavated. You can feel the difference on the page.

The behavior is the same in both versions: she volunteers. But the psychology in the second version has depth and complexity and contradiction in it. She's not noble. She's driven. There's a difference.

Three Markers of Discovery

How do you actually do this? How do you move from invention to discovery in your own work? I'll give you three markers to watch for.

The first is resistance. When you're getting close to something real in a character's psychology, you'll often feel a pull toward a safer, more comfortable version. The instinct will offer you something more defensible, more socially acceptable, more narratively conventional. That pull toward the safer version is useful information. It tells you you're near something that actually has charge.

The second is specificity that arrives uninvited. You're thinking about a character's past, and a detail surfaces that you didn't plan. A specific object. A specific room. A specific face. You don't know where it came from, but it feels right in a way that your planned details don't. That uninvited specificity is discovery.

The third is when the character starts behaving in ways that complicate your plot. When what they would actually do — given what you now know about them — creates problems for the story you intended to tell. That friction is almost always a sign that something true has surfaced.

Invented characters cooperate. Discovered characters insist.

What You Already Know

You know more about your character than you think you do. The information is already there — in the scenes you've written, in the choices you've made intuitively, in the moments that have stayed with you even when you couldn't explain why. The work isn't to manufacture a psychology from scratch.

The work is to learn to access what you already know.

That's the difference between excavation and fabrication. Between finding and building. And that difference — over the course of a novel — is the difference between a character a reader forgets and a character a reader carries with them.

The wound doesn't arrive through reasoning alone. The methodology uses three stages in sequence — each one opening something the previous one cannot access.

1
Deductive Reasoning

2

The wound should be proportional to the belief — not to what would make compelling backstory. The smallest experience sufficient to produce this specific conviction is often the most authentic one. Precisely Sized
