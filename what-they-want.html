I want to tell you about a client I worked with for three years.

He was the head of operations for a mid-size manufacturing company. Brilliant. Relentless. The kind of leader who arrived before anyone else and stayed after everyone had gone home.

He told me what he wanted. He wanted to build something that would last past him. Something that wouldn't fall apart when he left. He wanted the company to be strong enough to survive without his constant attention.

That was what he said. And he meant every word of it.

But here's what he did. He scheduled himself into every decision. He reviewed every report before it went out. He built systems, yes — and then positioned himself at the center of every one of them. His team was excellent. He told them so constantly. And then he made it structurally impossible for them to operate without his sign-off.

After about six months of working together, I asked him a question he didn't like.

"If your team became fully autonomous tomorrow — if they no longer needed you — what would happen?"

He thought it was a practical question. He started talking about succession planning. I stopped him.

"That's not what I'm asking. I'm asking what would happen to you."

He went quiet for a long time.

That's the moment this lesson is about.

Because what my client wanted — genuinely, consciously, out loud — was a self-sufficient organization. But what he was actually pursuing, every single day, was indispensability. The experience of being needed. Of being the one person whose absence would be felt.

The want was one thing. The need was something else entirely. And those two things were on a collision course he couldn't see — because the collision was invisible. It was happening inside him.

That's what we're doing today. We're finding the collision inside your character.

The Cognitive Shift

In Lesson One, you were a documentary filmmaker. You captured what happens on camera — specific, observable, recurring behavior.

In Lesson Two, you became a therapist. You asked what your character must believe for that behavior to feel necessary.

In Lesson Three, you became an archaeologist. You dug back to the event that formed that belief.

In Lesson Four, you were a forensic analyst. You identified the distortion — the conclusion your character drew from the wound that now functions as a law about who they are.

Today, you become a writer. Everything in Lessons One through Four has been excavation. You've been working inward, going deeper, peeling back layers of rationalization to find psychological truth. That work was essential. But it was contained — inside the character, outside the story.

What we're doing now connects the inside to the outside. The want and the need are where psychology becomes plot. They are the mechanism by which everything you've discovered about your character's inner life actually drives the story forward. This is where the excavation starts earning its keep.

What the Want Actually Is

Let me be precise about what the want is — because it is not the same as a goal.

A goal is external. Win the case. Escape the city. Get the promotion. Save the child. Goals are plot machinery — they're what your character is trying to accomplish in the world.

The want is something different. The want is a psychological strategy. It's what your character is pursuing beneath the surface goal — the thing they believe will finally make them feel safe, or whole, or worthy.

Here's how it forms. Your character drew a distorted conclusion from their wound. That's the lie. And the want is the lie in action. It's the behavioral strategy that emerges when someone tries to live out that lie and make it feel true.

If the lie is "I am not capable of being loved for who I am," the want becomes: to achieve enough that people have to love them. To earn through performance what they can't believe would be given freely.

If the lie is "vulnerability is the same as destruction," the want becomes control. To manage every situation so completely that nothing can touch them.

Notice what those wants have in common. They're not irrational. Given the lie, they're entirely logical. The want is the character's best attempt at a solution — to a problem that's actually unsolvable from inside the lie. That's the trap. And that trap is your story.

What the Need Actually Is

If the want is built on the lie, the need is built on the wound. More specifically, the need is what the wound took away — what was absent in the moment the wound was formed.

Every wound is, at its core, a deprivation. Something was present that shouldn't have been — threat, cruelty, betrayal, abandonment. Or something was absent that should have been — safety, love, protection, the experience of being seen. The need is the thing that was absent. The thing your character has been trying to recover, in various misdirected ways, ever since.

Here is the most important thing I'll say in this lesson: your character cannot simply choose the need.

This is where writers go wrong. They identify the need — connection, trust, belonging, love without conditions — and wonder why their character doesn't just pursue it. It seems so obvious from outside. But the need threatens the lie. And the lie feels like survival.

So the character resists the need. Actively, often unconsciously. They see the thing they need — it arrives in the form of a person, an opportunity, a moment of genuine connection — and they find a reason to reject it. Misread it. Destroy it. That resistance is not a character flaw. It is entirely human. And it is the engine of your entire story.

Katniss — All Five Layers

Let me show you how this lands with Katniss Everdeen.

Layer One: Her surface behavior. She protects. Compulsively, repeatedly. She volunteers at the Reaping. She steps between Rue and the tribute hunting her. She keeps Peeta alive at direct cost to herself.

Layer Two: Her hidden truth. The people I love are vulnerable and cannot protect themselves without me.

Layer Three: Her wound. Her father died. And then her mother collapsed. An eleven-year-old looked around for an adult to depend on and found no one.

Layer Four: Her lie. I am solely responsible for everyone's survival. If I love someone and don't protect them, their destruction is my failure.

Now Layer Five. Given that lie, what would Katniss pursue to feel safe? Her want is to be the protector. To guarantee outcomes. To ensure that no one she loves ever ends up helpless, with nowhere to turn.

And her need? A child needed to be held. She needed someone to say: you are not responsible for this. You don't have to carry this alone. Katniss's need is to trust. To allow herself to be protected. To set down the weight she picked up at eleven.

Test the tension. Does pursuing the want prevent getting the need? Absolutely. Every time she positions herself as the protector, she reinforces the belief that she is the only thing standing between the people she loves and destruction. She proves the lie to herself again. And she makes it structurally impossible to receive care in return.

Peeta offers her exactly what she needs. He is capable. He is honest. He wants to protect her. And she spends most of three books refusing it — or accepting it just long enough to feel terrified and pull away. That's not a character flaw. That's a character.

The Two Tracks

The want is the visible story. It's what your reader follows on the surface — goal, obstacle, pursuit, crisis, resolution. It's the plot.

The need is the invisible story. It's what the reader feels beneath the surface — the longing, the resistance, the moments of almost, the cost of all that running. It's the meaning.

Great fiction runs both tracks simultaneously. Every scene does two things at once. It advances the want — your character pursuing the strategy the lie demands. And it complicates the need — creating moments where the thing they're running from gets closer, where the cost of the lie becomes visible, where the reader can see what the character cannot.

That double track is not a technique. It is a direct consequence of the psychological precision you've been building since Lesson One.

Carry this question with you before you open your session: your character is pursuing the want. What are they afraid would happen if they stopped? That fear is exactly where Lesson Six will begin.

The Structural Conflict

Does pursuing the want actively block access to the need?

Every time Katniss positions herself as the protector, she reinforces the belief that she is the only thing standing between the people she loves and destruction. She proves the lie to herself again — and she makes it structurally impossible to receive care in return. Peeta offers her exactly what she needs. And it's why she's still being discussed decades after those books were written.

How to Test Your Pair

Write your character's want and need side by side. Then ask: does pursuing the want block access to the need? This must be structural — not a circumstance that could be resolved, but a fundamental incompatibility between the strategies the want and need require.

Can your character pursue the want at full intensity and simultaneously open themselves to the need? If yes, you don't have the right pair yet. The want and need need to be genuine opposites at the psychological level — not just different priorities, but mutually exclusive strategies for existing in the world.

Diagnostic test: if your character got everything they wanted — completely achieved the want — would they be happy? If the answer is yes, you don't have the right want. The want built on a lie cannot deliver what the character actually needs. It can only deliver confirmation of the lie.

Before Your Coaching Session

Four Signs You Haven't Found the Right Pair Yet

Read these before you start. Each one describes a way this session goes sideways — and how to correct it in the moment.

Red Flag 1

Your want is a plot goal, not a psychological strategy. "She wants to win the Games" is a goal. "She wants to be the kind of person who can never be helpless again" is a want. If your want sounds like a plot point, ask what achieving it would mean — at the level of identity, not circumstance. The want is the answer to: what does getting this make me feel about who I am?

Red Flag 2

Your character would eagerly pursue the need if they knew it was available. Then it's not the need — it's another want. The genuine need is threatening. It would require the character to relinquish something the lie has convinced them they need to survive. If the need feels safe or obvious, keep digging.

Red Flag 3

The want and need can coexist. If you can imagine your character getting both simultaneously — without one undermining the other — they're not in genuine tension. Real want-need pairs are structurally incompatible. The strategies they require are mutually exclusive. If both can coexist, find a more essential pair.

Red Flag 4

The need is never actually available in your story. The need must be reachable — not easily, not without cost, but genuinely present. Your story must place it in your character's path, repeatedly. They must be able to reach it and choose, again and again, not to. If the need is never offered, there is no arc.
