I want to start with something that happened early in my corporate coaching career.

I was working with a senior executive — brilliant woman, led a team of two hundred people — and I asked her to describe a colleague who was causing problems. Someone who was derailing team decisions.

She didn't hesitate. She said: "He agrees in every meeting. Then he goes back to his office and does exactly what he was going to do anyway. And he's been doing it for fifteen years."

She didn't have a psychology degree. She hadn't run any assessments. But she had watched this person across dozens of situations, with different people, under different kinds of pressure. And she knew exactly what he did.

That's what you're going to do today. With your character.

The Library You Don't Know You Have

That kind of knowing has a name. Psychologists call it the adaptive unconscious — the part of your mind that processes patterns before your conscious awareness can catch up.

It's what lets you walk into a room and immediately sense the tension. It's what tells you, thirty seconds into meeting someone, whether they're being genuine. You didn't calculate that. You just knew.

Every person you've ever observed. Every relationship you've navigated. Every character you've read who felt completely real to you. It's all filed away in there — a massive, lifetime database of human behavior.

Your adaptive unconscious is the most powerful character development tool you have. And most writers never use it deliberately. That's what this course teaches you to do. Not to invent psychology. To excavate what you already know.

Where Most Character Work Goes Wrong

Most writers approach character development chronologically. They start at the beginning — childhood, backstory, formative trauma — and build forward. They construct a psychology and attach it to a character.

The result often feels exactly like what it is: constructed. It fits the plot. It doesn't feel like a person.

We're going the other direction. We start with what your character does right now. Observable, specific, present-day behavior. From there, we dig down — layer by layer — to the belief that drives that behavior, the wound that created that belief, and the distorted conclusion they drew from the wound.

Surface to depth. Present to past. Observable to psychological. That's the excavation ladder. And today, you're climbing the first rung.

Today's Work: Pure Observation

Today, you're being a documentary filmmaker. You're capturing what happens on camera. No interpretation. No analysis. Just behavior.

Think about Katniss Everdeen. What does she do? She volunteers at the Reaping. She steps between danger and the people she loves — with Prim, with Rue, with Peeta. She positions herself as protector, again and again, in different situations, with different people, under different kinds of pressure. That's what you'd see on film.

Or Sherlock Holmes. What does he do? He examines. He picks up objects others ignore. He studies faces, clothing, hands. Every scene, every case — the same compulsion, expressing itself differently depending on context.

Notice what those descriptions aren't. They're not labels. They're not "she's protective" or "he's observant." They're specific actions. Things you can write. Things you can film. That's what you're finding today.

The Four Requirements

A strong surface behavior has four characteristics. And I want to be direct with you: these aren't suggestions. If your character's behavior doesn't meet all four, keep digging.

Specific. Not a label — a concrete action. Not "she's anxious." What does anxious look like? Does she check her phone repeatedly in social situations? Does she apologize before stating any opinion? Get specific.

Visible. Put a camera on your character. Would this show up? "She feels insecure" doesn't pass the test. "She apologizes before stating any opinion" does.

Recurring. This isn't a one-time reaction. It's a pattern across relationships, contexts, and time. Patterns signal psychology.

Consequential. This behavior affects relationships, decisions, the trajectory of their life. It creates conflict or shapes how others respond to them. It matters.

These four work together. Specificity makes it writable. Visibility makes it cinematic. Recurrence makes it meaningful. Consequence makes it story-worthy.

Supplemental · The Methodology Behavior vs. Trait — The Camera Test

I want to teach you a skill that will change how you see every character you ever write. It's a diagnostic. A one-question test you can run on any description of a character — yours or anyone else's — that tells you immediately whether you're working with something real or something that only sounds real.

The question is this: could a camera film it? That's it. That's the whole test. And it's harder to pass than it sounds.

Most writers describe their characters with traits. She's guarded. He's ambitious. She's self-destructive. He's charming but unreliable. These descriptions feel accurate. They might even be accurate. But none of them pass the camera test, because a camera can't film guarded. It can't film ambitious. A camera can only film what someone does — specific actions, in specific moments, with specific people.

That's the difference between a trait and a behavior. A trait tells you what a character is like. A behavior shows you what they actually do. And only a behavior gives you something to excavate.

Making the Conversion

Take a trait — any trait. Let's use guarded. Now ask: what does guarded actually look like? What does a guarded person do in conversation, in conflict, in moments of intimacy? What specific action would you see on film? Maybe they change the subject whenever someone asks about the past. Maybe they answer personal questions with questions of their own. Maybe they go physically still when a conversation gets too close — a pause before they speak, a slight turn of the body. Each of those is a behavior. Each of them passes the camera test.

Now take ambitious. What does ambitious look like? Does your character take credit for other people's work? Do they agree with whoever is in the room and then pursue their own agenda the moment the room empties? Do they reframe every social situation as an opportunity before the other person has finished speaking? Trait to behavior. Vague to specific. Abstract to observable.

This is what the excavation requires. Not the word, but the action underneath the word.

Katniss and Sherlock

Katniss Everdeen. One word: protective. That's the trait. Accurate, but it gives you nothing to work with. Now put the camera on her. She volunteers for Prim at the Reaping. She steps between Rue and the tribute who's hunting her. She keeps Peeta alive in the arena at direct cost to her own survival. She positions herself between danger and the people she loves — again and again, with different people, under different kinds of pressure. That pattern of specific actions is the behavior. And that behavior is something you can follow all the way down to the psychology that drives it.

Sherlock Holmes. One word: observant. Everyone says it. Now put the camera on him. He picks up objects other people walk past without seeing. He studies the position of someone's hands, the wear pattern on their shoes, the small tells in their face before they speak. He connects details in ways no one else sees coming. Every scene, the same compulsion — expressing itself differently depending on what the situation requires. That's not a trait. That's a pattern of behavior. And a pattern of behavior has a source.

Why This Distinction Matters

Readers don't experience traits. They experience behaviors. They don't read "she was guarded" and feel anything. They watch her turn away, change the subject, keep her eyes on the exit — and they feel it. The behavior is what creates the experience of the character. The trait is just a shortcut writers use when they haven't yet found the behavior underneath it.

The next time you write a description of your character — even a note in your own journal — stop at every adjective. Every word that ends in -ed or -ive or -ous. Guarded. Ambitious. Protective. Anxious. Defensive. Run the camera test. What does that actually look like? What would you see on film? Write the behavior instead of the label. One specific action. One thing the camera could capture.

Do that consistently, and your characters will stop feeling like descriptions of people. They'll start feeling like people. That's what this methodology is built on. And that's what you're learning to do.
