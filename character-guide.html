Choosing Your Character

This guide has one purpose: to ensure you bring the right character into the excavation. Work through it before you open Lesson 1. The fifteen minutes you spend here will shape every session that follows.

Why This Decision Matters

The quality of your STRATUM experience depends almost entirely on which character you choose to excavate. Not on your writing ability. Not on how long you've been working on your story. On this one decision.

Writers who arrive at Lesson 1 with the wrong character — one that's too thin, too new, or too emotionally unformed — find the sessions feel like invention rather than discovery. The chain holds together logically. But it doesn't feel true. That gap between plausible and true is everything.

This guide gives you three markers to evaluate your choice: a decision framework that tells you whether your character is ready for excavation, five diagnostic questions that surface what you already know about them, and a clear explanation of how the written versus unwritten distinction changes what the methodology requires of you.

Work through each section in order. Don't skip to the checklist. The thinking matters as much as the conclusion.

The Methodology

The Core Requirement

STRATUM excavates existing psychological truth. It cannot create it. Understanding this distinction is the most important thing you can know before Lesson 1 begins.

What Excavation Means — And Doesn't

Every lesson in this course is built on a single operating principle: the psychology of a well-formed character already exists. It exists in your unconscious before you can articulate it. It exists in the choices you've already made for your character — the things they do, the things they avoid, the way they respond to pressure — even if you haven't traced those choices to their origin.

STRATUM is the process of tracing them. Not inventing them. Tracing what is already present, even if half-formed, even if only intuited, until it becomes explicit enough to write from.

If there is nothing there to find — if your character is a placeholder, a set of plot functions without genuine psychological depth — the sessions will not reveal that depth. They will construct it. And constructed psychology feels exactly like what it is: assembled. Logically coherent. Not true.

The Two Categories
01 Ready for Excavation

Your character has been with you long enough to develop genuine interiority. You sense things about them you haven't fully explained yet. When you imagine them in a new situation, something specific and consistent happens — not what would be convenient for the plot, but what they would actually do.

The signal: You sense a locked room. Something present, but not yet accessible. You know it's there. You can't quite get to it yet.

02 Not Ready Yet

Your character was created to serve the plot. Their traits are defined by what the story needs rather than who they are. When you imagine them in a new situation, you find yourself asking what would be useful — not what they would do.

The signal: You can describe what they look like and what role they play, but not how they would react to something that has nothing to do with the story's events.

The Locked Room

The phrase I use for the signal of a ready character is a locked room. Something present in the character that you can sense but not yet fully access. A quality, a pattern, a contradiction you've noticed but haven't explained. A history you've intuited but haven't written.

That lock isn't a problem to solve before you begin. It is the point of beginning. The locked room is exactly what STRATUM is designed to open — layer by layer, from the outside in. What your character does, what they believe, what formed that belief, what they concluded about themselves from it.

If you sense the room, you're ready. That feeling — of knowing something is there without being able to say yet what it is — is the exact condition the methodology is built for.

The Decision Framework

Who To Bring

Not every character is ready for excavation. This framework helps you identify which character in your story is the right one to bring — and why the choice matters more than most writers expect.

Bring

Your protagonist, if they have depth. A protagonist built around genuine psychology — not just plot function — is almost always the right choice. They carry the most weight in the story, they appear across the widest range of situations, and you've likely spent the most time with them. If you sense the locked room in your protagonist, start there.

Bring

The character you keep thinking about. Sometimes the character that won't leave you alone is not the protagonist. It might be a supporting character, an antagonist, a secondary figure who keeps revealing unexpected dimensions. If someone other than your protagonist has the locked room quality, bring them.

Reconsider

A character you invented recently. Characters who have existed for only a few weeks — especially those created to solve a plot problem — rarely have enough psychological sediment for meaningful excavation. The methodology requires something to find. Give them time first.

Reconsider

A character defined entirely by their function. The mentor. The best friend. The obstacle. If you can describe your character almost entirely in terms of what they do for your protagonist, their psychology may not be developed enough yet. Function is not psychology.

Reconsider

A character you built by filling out a worksheet. If most of what you know about this character came from answering prompt questions — personality type, backstory bullet points, a list of flaws — rather than from observation of how they behave in your imagination, the psychology may be more constructed than discovered. That's a different kind of work.

The Second Marker

Five Diagnostic Questions

Run your character through these five questions honestly. They are not warm-up exercises. They are diagnostic. If you cannot produce even a partial answer to most of them, that is information — and it is telling you something important about your character's readiness.

Question 1

What does your character do when they're afraid — not in the plot's big moments, but in ordinary life? This is not asking about the climax. It's asking about the pattern. How does their fear show up when nothing significant is at stake? If you can answer this with a specific, consistent behavior, your character has psychology.

Question 2

What does your character want that they would never ask for directly? Most characters are clearer about what they pursue openly than about what they reach for sideways. The indirect want — what they circle around, approach through other means, or deny wanting entirely — reveals more about psychology than the stated goal.

Question 3

What would your character do if they were alone, with no one watching and no consequences? The behavior that exists only when there's an audience is not the deepest behavior. What happens when the performance stops? If you can picture something specific — not virtuous, not convenient, just true — your character is ready.

Question 4

What has your character decided about other people that shapes how they move through the world? Not a stated belief — an operating conclusion. Something like: people who get close eventually disappear. Or: kindness is always transactional. Or: the only person I can rely on is myself. This conclusion doesn't have to be conscious. It just has to be consistent.

Question 5

What does your character do that surprises even you? This is the most important question. If your character has ever done something in your imagination — made a choice, delivered a line, refused something you expected them to accept — that you didn't plan but recognized as exactly right, they are generating their own logic. That is the signal of genuine psychological depth.

How to Read Your Answers

You don't need to answer all five fully. But if you stall on four of the five — if the questions feel not difficult but unanswerable — pause before moving to Lesson 1. That stall is diagnostic. It doesn't mean your character isn't interesting. It means they may not yet be formed enough for excavation to find what isn't there.

If you can answer two or three with something specific — even partial, even uncertain — proceed. The methodology will help you develop the rest. Partial answers are the starting point, not the obstacle.

The Third Marker

Written vs Unwritten

Whether you have manuscript pages or only notes changes what the STRATUM methodology requires of you. This is something most character development courses ignore entirely. It matters here.

Your Situation
01 You Have Pages

If you have manuscript pages — even a first draft, even something rough — your character has already made choices on the page. Those choices are your primary data. The methodology asks you to pull from what's written, not what you intended or planned.

Your approach: In your coaching sessions, lead with what's on the page. When your coach asks what your character does, start with examples from your manuscript. The scenes you've already written contain the surface pattern waiting to be identified.

02 You Have Notes

If you're working from notes, outlines, or planning documents — if no scenes have been written yet — the methodology asks you to work from imagination rather than evidence. You'll be observing what your character does in your mind's eye rather than on the page.

Your approach: When your coach asks what your character does, imagine them in specific situations. Trust what arrives. Your adaptive unconscious has been building this character even without pages — the knowledge is there, just less formalized.

Why This Distinction Matters

Writers with pages sometimes struggle in the sessions because they try to reconcile what they intended with what they actually wrote. When a character makes an unexpected choice on the page, that choice is data — not an error to explain away. The page is the primary source. Intention is secondary.

Writers without pages sometimes struggle because they reach for what would be useful for the story rather than what is true for the character. The discipline here is the same as for writers with pages: observe, don't construct. Ask what arrives, not what would be convenient.

In both situations, your job in the sessions is the same: report what you observe, not what you think you should observe. The methodology does the rest.

A Common Situation

More Than One

Many writers arrive at this guide with two or three characters who seem equally ready for excavation. If that's you, this section is for you. The answer is almost always clearer than it feels.

The Test That Settles It

When two characters both seem ready, apply this test: Which one do you know less about?

That answer is usually immediate — and it's almost always the right character to bring. The one you know less about is the one with more to find. The one you already understand fully isn't waiting for excavation; you've done that work in your imagination already, even if you haven't formalized it.

The character who still has questions attached to them — the one where you sense something you haven't figured out — is the one the methodology is designed for.

If Both

Both feel equally unknown. Choose the one who carries more weight in the story — the one whose psychological truth will have the most structural consequence. An excavated protagonist changes the entire story. An excavated secondary character changes their scenes. Both are valuable. The protagonist is usually the higher leverage choice.

If One

One is the protagonist and one is someone else. Start with the protagonist unless the secondary character is clearly more psychologically developed. The protagonist's excavated architecture shapes every scene, every relationship, every decision. That's usually where the work has the most payoff.

Important

Bring one character into this course. Not two. The methodology is designed for depth on one character — going through all eight layers of a single psychology. Splitting your focus across two characters means you'll excavate neither one fully. Make the choice now and commit to it.

Before You Begin

Emotional Distance

The last thing to consider before you make your final choice is your relationship to the character you're bringing. STRATUM requires a particular kind of access — and too little or too much distance gets in the way.

What the Right Distance Looks Like

The right emotional distance means you care about the character enough to think carefully about them — but you're not so identified with them that their failures feel like your failures, or their wounds feel like yours.

Writers who are too close to a character often protect them. They soften the diagnostic questions, avoid the darker answers, steer away from wounds that feel too personal. The sessions become exercises in affirmation rather than excavation.

Writers who are too distant — who bring a character they don't particularly care about, or one they've been working on so long that the relationship has gone cold — often find the sessions feel mechanical. The right questions arrive, but nothing lands. The discoveries don't register.

The right distance is: you're curious about this person. You want to understand them. You're not afraid of what you might find.

Too Close

Your character is significantly autobiographical. If this character is closely based on yourself — if their wounds are your wounds, their history is your history — proceed with care. The methodology can still work, but the risk of projection is high. You may find yourself excavating your own psychology rather than theirs. That's a different kind of work, and a different kind of readiness is required for it.

Too Distant

You don't particularly care what you find. If the character feels like an assignment rather than a relationship — if you're bringing them because they're the protagonist and not because you're genuinely curious about them — consider whether a different character might be more alive for you right now. The sessions work best when something is at stake in the discovery.

Right Distance

You're curious and slightly uncertain. You know enough to begin, but there's something about this character you haven't figured out yet. You want to. You're not afraid of what you might find, but you don't already know the answer. That combination — knowledge plus genuine question — is exactly what the methodology is built for.
