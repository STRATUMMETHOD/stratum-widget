Before Lesson 1 begins, you need to make one decision. Not about your plot. Not about your structure. About your character.

Which character you bring to this course determines almost everything that follows — the quality of the work, the depth you can reach, and whether the methodology reveals something true about your character or simply confirms what you already assumed.

Most writers think they already know the answer. Sometimes they are right. But the writers who get the most from this course are the ones who made that choice deliberately — not by default. This guide exists to make it deliberate.

There are three markers I want you to track as you work through it.

The first marker: a decision framework. Not every character is ready for excavation. Some are too thin — built for plot rather than psychology. Others are ready but require a particular kind of awareness. The framework will tell you which category your character is in, and what that means for the work ahead.

The second marker: five diagnostic questions. These are not warm-up exercises. Run your character through them honestly. If you cannot produce even a partial answer to most of them, that is information. It means the character is not ready — or you are not ready to excavate this particular one yet.

And the third marker: the written versus unwritten distinction. Whether you have manuscript pages or only notes changes what the methodology requires of you. The guide will tell you exactly how.

Here is what I need you to understand before you open Lesson 1. STRATUM excavates existing psychological truth. It cannot create it. The methodology works by finding what is already present in your character — even if half-formed, even if only intuited.

If there is nothing there to find, the sessions will feel like construction. Everything will seem plausible. The chain will hold together. But it will not feel true. It will feel built.

The right character has what I call a locked room. Something present, but not yet accessible. You sense it is there. You cannot quite get to it. That is the signal. That is who you bring.

Spend fifteen minutes with this guide before you open Course Reference Materials. Work through the checklist at the end. By the time you reach the final question, you will know exactly who you are bringing into the excavation, and why. Fifteen minutes here will save you hours of friction later.

I
The Core Requirement
What every qualifying character must have

STRATUM excavates existing psychological truth. The methodology cannot build psychology from scratch. It can only find what is already present, even if half-formed, even if only intuited.

Your character needs to meet one foundational requirement: they must do something you have noticed but cannot yet explain. A recurring behavior that feels weighted. A choice that surprised you when you wrote it. A pattern you sense is connected to something deeper.

The Right Signal

You do not need to understand your character to bring them to this course. You need to feel that there is something to understand. If you sense a locked room — something present but not yet accessible — that is the signal. The methodology opens locked rooms. It cannot create them.

III
The Five Diagnostic Questions
Run your candidate through each before committing

For each character you are considering, work through these questions honestly. A strong candidate will produce at least partial answers to all five.

  • 1
    Can you name one thing this character does repeatedly — specific enough that a camera could film it?Not a personality trait (“she is guarded”) — a specific observable action (“she ends phone calls before the other person does”). If you cannot find one now, begin in observation before the course does.
  • 2
    Has this character ever done something that surprised you when you wrote it — or that you cannot fully explain?Surprise is evidence of genuine psychology operating beneath conscious construction. The unexplained moment is the door the methodology opens.
  • 3
    When you imagine this character's worst internal moment, do you sense what it costs them — even if you cannot articulate it?You do not need to know what it costs. You need to sense that something is there. Pure blankness suggests a character built for plot rather than psychology.
  • 4
    Does this character want something they might not be able to have — and something else underneath that they will not admit?This is a preview of Lesson 5. No gap between surface want and deep need means no story engine for STRATUM to build from.
  • 5
    If their deepest pattern were exposed — if they could no longer hide what drives them — what would break open?The answer to this question is the story. If you cannot feel any answer forming, even vague or partial, this character may not yet be ready.
Scoring

Strong answers on 4 or 5: Bring this character.  Strong answers on 2 or 3: Proceed with awareness — the missing answers are where you will feel the most friction.  Strong answers on 1 or fewer: Consider a different character, or spend more time in observation before Lesson 1.

IV
Written vs. Unwritten
What each requires of you
✓ Written CharacterYou have manuscript pages or substantial scenes

You have evidence. The manuscript acts as a check against fabrication — when something feels invented, test it against what you have already written. That grounding is a significant advantage across all eight lessons.

⚠ Unwritten CharacterExists in notes or imagination only

Every answer feels plausible because nothing contradicts it. Internal consistency is not the same as psychological truth. Expect to work harder on the discovery/invention distinction throughout every session.

The Unwritten Character Risk

The most common failure mode: everything holds together, the chain feels tight, the wound makes sense — but it was all constructed to be consistent, not discovered to be true. If every answer arrives without resistance or surprise, apply extra skepticism.

V
When More Than One Character Is Calling
Three tiebreakers, in order
  • →
    Which character is most central to the story's emotional core? Not the most active — the one whose psychological transformation the story cannot happen without.
  • →
    Which character do you understand least? The one whose choices occasionally surprise or unsettle you usually has more genuine psychology to find. A character you fully understand may have been built rather than discovered.
  • →
    Which character, if you finally understood their deepest wound, would most change how you write the story? That character has the most leverage. Bring them.
On Changing Characters Mid-Course

It happens. You begin and realize at Lesson 2 or 3 that you have brought the wrong character — or that this excavation has revealed a different character as the real psychological center of your story. This is not failure. It is the methodology working. What you have already excavated is not wasted.

VI
A Note on Emotional Distance
Your material, your terms

STRATUM excavates wounds — formative experiences that created beliefs your character has carried ever since. For some writers, working with a character's wound means working at a safe distance from their own material. For others, the distance narrows unexpectedly.

You are a storyteller, not a therapist in session. The excavation goes as deep as your story needs, and you control how deep that is.

If Your Character Carries Material Close to Your Own

You may choose any character for any reason. If a character feels too close, choosing a different one is not avoidance — it is craft judgment. The methodology does not require you to go anywhere that does not serve the story.

VII

  • If multiple characters were calling, I have used the tiebreaker framework and committed to one.
  • I understand that I am not building this character's psychology. I am finding what is already there.
